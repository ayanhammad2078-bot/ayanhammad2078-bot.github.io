How to Use the Compound Interest Calculator

  1. Enter your starting principal — the initial amount you invest or deposit.
  2. Enter the annual interest rate as a percentage.
  3. Enter the time period in years for which the money will be invested.
  4. Select how often interest is compounded (monthly is most common for savings accounts).
  5. Optionally, enter a monthly contribution to simulate regular deposits.
  6. Click Calculate to see your final balance, total interest earned, and total contributions.

Understanding Compound Interest

The compound interest formula is: A = P(1 + r/n)^(nt), where A is the final amount, P is the principal, r is the annual interest rate (decimal), n is the number of times interest compounds per year, and t is the time in years. More frequent compounding results in slightly higher returns.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is compound interest?

Compound interest is interest calculated on both the initial principal and the accumulated interest from previous periods. Unlike simple interest, which is only calculated on the principal, compound interest grows exponentially over time.

How does compounding frequency affect returns?

The more frequently interest compounds, the more you earn. Daily compounding produces slightly more than monthly, which produces more than annual. The difference becomes more significant over longer time periods and with higher interest rates.

What is the Rule of 72?

The Rule of 72 is a quick way to estimate how long it takes to double your money. Simply divide 72 by the annual interest rate. For example, at 8% interest, your money doubles in approximately 72 ÷ 8 = 9 years.

Does compound interest apply to debt too?

Yes. Compound interest works against you when you carry debt. Credit card balances, for example, compound monthly, which is why high-interest debt can grow rapidly if not paid off. This is why paying off debt quickly is financially advantageous.
