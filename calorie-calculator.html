Calorie Calculator

The Calorie Calculator estimates your Total Daily Energy Expenditure (TDEE) — the number of calories your body needs each day based on your age, gender, height, weight, and activity level. Knowing your daily calorie needs is the first step toward achieving your health goals, whether that is losing weight, gaining muscle, or simply maintaining your current weight.

How to Use the Calorie Calculator

  1. Select your gender (biological sex affects the BMR formula).
  2. Enter your age in years.
  3. Enter your weight in kilograms and height in centimeters.
  4. Select your activity level that best matches your typical week.
  5. Choose your goal: maintain, lose, or gain weight.
  6. Click Calculate Calories to see your recommended daily intake.

Understanding Your Calorie Result

This calculator uses the Mifflin-St Jeor equation to estimate your Basal Metabolic Rate (BMR) — the calories your body needs at rest — and then multiplies it by an activity factor to get your TDEE. Your goal adjustment adds or subtracts calories to create a deficit or surplus for weight change. A deficit of 500 cal/day typically results in approximately 0.5 kg (1 lb) of weight loss per week.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a calorie?

A calorie (technically a kilocalorie, or kcal) is a unit of energy. In nutrition, it refers to the amount of energy provided by food and beverages. Your body uses calories to fuel all its functions, from breathing to physical activity.

What is BMR?

BMR stands for Basal Metabolic Rate. It is the number of calories your body needs to maintain basic physiological functions at complete rest — such as breathing, circulation, and cell production. BMR accounts for roughly 60–75% of your total daily calorie expenditure.

How many calories should I eat to lose weight?

A general guideline is to create a calorie deficit of 500 calories per day, which leads to approximately 0.5 kg (1 lb) of weight loss per week. Deficits larger than 1000 cal/day are not recommended without medical supervision, as they can lead to nutrient deficiencies.

Are these calorie estimates exact?

No. These are estimates based on population averages. Individual metabolism varies due to genetics, hormones, muscle mass, and other factors. Use these numbers as a starting point and adjust based on your actual results over 2–4 weeks.

What is TDEE?

TDEE stands for Total Daily Energy Expenditure. It represents the total number of calories you burn in a day, including your BMR plus calories burned through physical activity and digestion (thermic effect of food).
