How to Use the Loan Calculator

  1. Enter the total loan amount you wish to borrow.
  2. Enter the annual interest rate offered by your lender.
  3. Enter the loan term in years (e.g., 5, 10, 30).
  4. Select the loan type: amortized (standard) or interest-only.
  5. Click Calculate Loan to see your monthly payment, total interest, and total cost.

Understanding Your Loan Results

Monthly Payment is the fixed amount you pay each month. Total Interest is the extra amount you pay on top of the principal over the life of the loan. Total Cost is the sum of the principal and all interest paid. A lower interest rate or shorter term reduces your total cost significantly.

Frequently Asked Questions

How is a monthly loan payment calculated?

For an amortized loan, the formula is: M = P[r(1+r)^n] / [(1+r)^n - 1], where P is the principal, r is the monthly interest rate, and n is the number of payments. Our calculator handles this automatically.

What is the difference between an amortized and interest-only loan?

In an amortized loan, each payment covers both interest and principal, so the balance decreases over time. In an interest-only loan, you only pay interest for a set period, meaning the principal does not reduce until you start making principal payments.

Does a higher interest rate always mean a worse deal?

Generally yes — a higher rate means you pay more in total interest. However, loan fees, prepayment penalties, and other terms also affect the true cost. Always compare the Annual Percentage Rate (APR) across lenders.

Can I use this for a mortgage calculation?

Yes. Enter your home loan amount, the mortgage interest rate, and the term (typically 15 or 30 years) to estimate your monthly mortgage payment. Note that actual mortgage payments may also include property taxes and insurance (escrow).

What happens if I make extra payments?

Making extra payments reduces your principal faster, which lowers the total interest you pay and can shorten your loan term. This calculator shows the standard payment schedule; consult your lender for prepayment options.
